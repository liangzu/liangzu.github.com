Have a try

Type or copy the following code into your REPL to see what will happen and you can obtain an intuitive idea what are eq?, eqv? and equal?.

eq?

(define ls-a (cons 1 '(2 3)))
(define ls-b (cons 1 '(2 3)))
(define ls-c ls-a)
(eq? (cons 1 '(2 3)) (cons 1 '(2 3)))
(eq? ls-a '(cons 1 '(2 3)))
(eq? ls-a ls-b)
(eq? ls-a ls-c)

eqv?

(eqv? (+ 2 3) (- 10 5))
(eqv? 5 6)
(eqv? 5 'five)
(eqv? 'cat 'cat)
(eqv? 'cat 'kitten)
(eqv? (car '(a a a )) (car (cdr '(a a a))))

equal?

(equal? 3 (/ 6 2))
(equal? 'cat 'cat)
(equal? '(a b c) (cons 'a '(b c)))
(equal? (cons 1'(2 3)) (cons 1 '(2 3)))
(equal? '(a (b c) d) '(a (b c) d))
(equal? '(a (b c)) '(a (c b)))
(equal? (cdr '(a c d)) (cdr '(b c d)))

What do they even mean?

eq?
The predicate eq? returns #t if its two arguments are identical in all respects; otherwise it returns #f.
eqv?
When we want to include numbers, symbols and booleans in the types of objects the predicate tests for sameness, we can use the predicate eqv?.
equal?
If we want a universal sameness predicate that can be applied to test numbers, symbols, booleans, procedures, and lists(and strings, characters, and vectors), we use the predicate equal?.

How dow we know which one to use?

  • for numbers, use =;
  • for symbols, use eq?;
